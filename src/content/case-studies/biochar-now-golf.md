## Why this case study sits on our site

The 50% irrigation-savings figure for biochar on golf courses comes up regularly in conversation. We want to be clear: that figure belongs to **Biochar Now**, a US biochar producer based in Colorado. It is not a Possible Agriculture result, and we don't claim it.

We include it here because:

1. The mechanism is sound — biochar at meaningful inclusion rates in a sand-profile root zone does substantially increase water retention, and the magnitude is plausible in California's irrigation context.
2. The product profile is broadly similar to ours — high-temperature pyrolysis, activated, hardwood feedstock.
3. Pretending the figure doesn't exist would be silly; better to give it its proper attribution and let course superintendents draw their own conclusions about what's transferable.

## The result, as Biochar Now report it

Biochar Now's published case study reports up to 50% reduction in irrigation water on a California golf course after biochar amendment to fairway and rough root zones. The result is specific to that site, that product, that climate, and that irrigation baseline.

## What this means for an Australian course considering biochar

The Possible Agriculture-specific number you should plan against is **up to 40% irrigation savings in trials where our biochar has been applied** — our own anchor, from our own brochure data. Anything above that is upside; treat it as evidence-based optimism rather than a commitment.

For a serious procurement conversation, the peer-reviewed work to focus on is Yu et al. 2013 (water-holding doubling at 9% biochar in sandy soils) — see our [biochar library](/library).
