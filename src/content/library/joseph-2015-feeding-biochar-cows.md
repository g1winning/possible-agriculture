A 53-hectare field study in Western Australia by some of the heaviest hitters in Australian biochar research — Stephen Joseph (UNSW), Lukas Van Zwieten (NSW DPI), and Zakaria Solaiman (UWA), among others. The trial design is the kind of farm-systems thinking that's hard to argue with: feed the cattle biochar+molasses, let the dung beetles do the spreading and burial work for free.

The mechanism: biochar passes through the rumen and gut more or less intact, comes out in the dung now loaded with nutrients and microbial life, and dung beetles transport the package down to 20–60 cm in the soil profile where it stays stable for decades to centuries.

For Australian dairy and grazing operators with active dung beetle populations, this is the highest-leverage way to put biochar in the ground. The carbon stays put, the pasture improves, and you didn't have to lift a spreader.
