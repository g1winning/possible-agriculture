A NSW South Coast trial comparing three fire regimes — agency-led hazard-reduction burns, cool-fire burns, and unburnt control sites — and measuring the resulting soil-carbon and nitrogen content.

The mechanism is what matters for biochar production: lower combustion temperatures preserve charred biomass in the soil profile rather than gasifying it to CO₂. The 1.7× SOC differential between cool-fire and unburnt sites is direct empirical evidence of how much char survives at the right temperature window.

The same combustion principle is what the flame-curtain method controls for at industrial scale: pyrolysis temperatures held to a specification that maximises char retention and minimises emissions. Where the paper's cool-fire approach is uncontrolled and uneven across a landscape, the flame-curtain method produces a measured, repeatable carbon output by the m³.
