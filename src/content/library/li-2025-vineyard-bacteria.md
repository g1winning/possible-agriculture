A 2025 paper that nails down the mechanism by which biochar makes microbial inoculants actually work. The porous biochar structure provides a "sheltering" microhabitat where beneficial bacteria can attach, colonise the rhizosphere, and resist competitive pressure from the existing microbial community.

The trial used Bacillus subtilis B4 and Pseudomonas resinovorans B9 — both producers of extracellular polysaccharides that help bind soil aggregates and tolerate salinity. With biochar, the bacteria stuck around long enough to reshape the bacterial community, lift soil structure, increase P and K availability, and significantly improve grape vine performance.

For Australian vineyards, orchards and pastures on saline-alkaline soils — particularly the irrigation-tail country in inland NSW, VIC and SA — this is the paper that justifies a biochar-plus-microbial-inoculant program over either input alone.
